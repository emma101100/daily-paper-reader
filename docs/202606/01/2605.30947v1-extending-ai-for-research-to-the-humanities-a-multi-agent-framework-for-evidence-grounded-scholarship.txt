Abstract 

LLM-based research agents have advanced rapidly in science and engineering, where re-search is organized around executable experi-ments, code, and quantitative signals. Human-ities scholarship, however, requires a differ-ent mode of reasoning: interpretive, evidence-grounded argument over primary sources, where scholarly value depends on faithful quo-tation, verifiable provenance, and close reading. Existing research agents remain largely opti-mized for execution and retrieval, not evidence-grounded interpretive reasoning. To address this gap, we introduce SPIRE (Scholarly-

Primitives-Inspired Research Engine), a multi-agent framework for evidence-grounded hu-manities scholarship. Drawing on Scholarly Primitives theory, SPIRE casts recurring hu-manities operations as cooperating agent roles (source discovery, evidence annotation, com-parison, provenance checking, sampling, ci-tation binding, and argumentative synthesis) over a multi-scale close-reading substrate of passages, intra-context graph communities, and cross-context semantic clusters. On a peer-reviewed-paper benchmark over classi-cal Chinese and Greco-Roman Latin scholar-ship, SPIRE recovers cited primary-source ev-idence more reliably than Naive LLM, Text RAG, and GraphRAG, and receives higher blind-judge scores on answer accuracy, depth, coverage, and evidence quality. Ablations show that both the scholarly-operation agents and close-reading retrieval contribute to evidence-grounded essays. Code, data catalogues, and reproduction scripts are released at https:// github.com/YatingPan/SPIRE .

1 Introduction 

LLM-based agents have recently achieved strong performance across scientific research domains. In 

natural sciences they design experiments, ana-lyze data, and test hypotheses (Boiko et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Ghafarollahi and Buehler, 2024; Romera-Paredes et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025b); in computer science they survey literature, write and test code, and plan experiments (Schmidgall et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Jimenez et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025; Zhuge et al., 2024); and in the social sciences they conduct quantitative analy-sis and simulate participants or populations (Park et al., 2023; Horton et al., 2023; Argyle et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Piao et al., 2025; Gao et al., 2024). These systems are enabled by advances in workflow orchestration (Wu et al., 2023), multi-agent collaboration (Chen et al., 2024b), retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2023c), chain-of-thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2022), reasoning–acting loops (Yao et al., 2023), self-reflection (Shinn et al., 2023), and re-inforcement learning (Guo et al., 2025). Across these domains, however, reseach is typically orga-nized around executable procedures, quantitative evaluation, or externally verifiable outcomes. Humanities research requires a different kind of research assistance. 1 Unlike scientific workflows, it does not primarily require executable computation or reproduction of quantitative results. Fluent parametric an-swers are therefore insufficient when they fail to quote, contextualize, and reason from textual evi-dence (Smith, 2016). Humanities scholarship is interpretive and open-ended, yet still organized around recurring schol-arly operations. Scholarly Primitives theory char-acterizes these operations as activities such as dis-covering, annotating, comparing, referring, sam-pling, illustrating, and representing (Unsworth, 2000). Building on this perspective, we opera-tionalize recurring humanities research practices as executable multi-agent workflows: SPIRE 

(Scholarly-Primitives-Inspired Research Engine) is a multi-agent framework in which agents cooper-ate over a typed EvidencePool to discover sources, annotate evidence, compare sides, check prove-nance, sample passages, bind citations, and synthe-size a citation-grounded argument. To our knowl-edge, SPIRE is among the first multi-agent re-search framework designed explicitly for evidence-grounded interpretive scholarship over primary sources, rather than isolated computational or digi-tal humanities tasks. Close reading depends on grounding interpreta-tion in faithful primary-source evidence. RAG and its variants improve text search by embedding pas-sages, and increasingly graphs or structured knowl-edge, for semantic retrieval (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2023c; Edge et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2025; Han et al., 2025). However, humanistic reading requires more than retrieval: a scholar does not merely select relevant passages, but situates them in various contexts, including explicit comparison with related works (Kristeva, 1980) and recurring conceptual formations across texts (Lovejoy, 1936). We therefore build a multi-scale close-reading sub-strate for the scholarly-primitive agents: passage embeddings for direct text evidence, entity-relation graph communities for intra-context relational evi-dence, and semantic clusters for cross-context re-current evidence. Evaluation should therefore prioritize evidence-groundedness and defensible interpretation rather than the reproduction of a quantitative result. To this end, we construct a peer-reviewed-paper bench-mark over classical Chinese and Greco-Roman Latin scholarship, extracting each paper’s research question, main findings, and cited primary-source evidence. Given only a research question, a sys-tem must perform the scholarly steps and pro-duce an evidence-grounded essay. We evaluate two complementary dimensions: cited-evidence recovery, testing whether the system retrieves pri-mary sources used in the reference paper, and blind scholarly evaluation by both expert human review-ers and LLM judgesassessing answer accuracy, depth, coverage, and evidence quality. Compared with Naive LLM, Text RAG, and GraphRAG base-2lines, SPIRE attains 44 .3% evidence recall versus 

≤22 .4% for the strongest baseline (roughly dou-ble) and receives higher judge scores on all four aspects. Ablations show that downgrading individ-ual curation primitives or close-reading retrieval tiers consistently reduces performance. Our contributions are as follows: 1. We introduce SPIRE , a multi-agent framework for evidence-grounded humanities scholarship that operationalizes Scholarly Primitives as co-ordinated agent workflows for interpretive re-search over primary sources. 2. We propose a multi-scale close-reading sub-strate combining passages, graph communities, and semantic clusters to support direct evidence grounding, contextual interpretation, and recur-rent conceptual analysis. 3. We construct a peer-reviewed-paper bench-mark of 406 papers centered on primary-source evidence and scholarly judging, providing a reusable testbed for future humanities research agents. 

2 Background and Related Work 

2.1 LLM agents for research and their evaluation 

LLM agents have emerged as a major paradigm for AI-assisted research (Zheng et al., 2025), and are most developed in domains with explicit opera-tional or verification signals. In the natural sciences agents connect LLMs with laboratory APIs and domain-specific tools to plan and execute experi-ments (Boiko et al., 2023; Bran et al., 2024); in ma-chine learning they automate parts of the research cycle, from ideation to code execution and paper writing (Lu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Schmidgall et al., 2025). Recent benchmarks such as Sci-enceAgentBench further formalize scientific dis-covery as executable workflows evaluated through code and execution (Chen et al., 2025b). Humanities research poses a different target: its output is not an executed program, reproduced ex-periment, or simulated population, but an evidence-grounded interpretation of primary sources. The required operations are close reading, evidence se-lection, citation, comparison, provenance checking, and argumentative synthesis. Evaluations based on task completion, code execution, numerical repro-duction, or generic NLG similarity therefore leave open how to design agents for interpretive scholar-ship, where success depends on evidence-grounded argument rather than executable verification. 

2.2 Retrieval-augmented generation for research 

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) grounds model outputs in retrieved passages to reduce un-supported claims and enable citation-backed an-swers (Lewis et al., 2020). For research, Pa-perQA answers over full-text articles (Lálá et al., 2023) and OpenScholar scales retrieval and self-feedback to millions of papers (Asai et al., 2024); graph-based variants add structured knowledge: GraphRAG via entity-relation graphs and commu-nity summaries, HyperGraphRAG via higher-order hyperedges (Edge et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2025). These have been applied across domains such as medicine (Wu et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2026) and technical documents (Knollmeyer et al., 2025). But humanities evidence use differs from sci-entific literature survey: it asks not which paper supports an answer, but which primary-source pas-sage, section, quotation, or summary bears on an interpretation. Standard RAG retrieves semanti-cally similar passages and graph RAG expands to structural knowledge; neither models primary-source provenance as the basis of an interpretive argument. Our evidence metric adapts attributed text gener-ation , which scores how faithfully claims are sup-ported by cited sources (Bohnet et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023a,b), but specializes it to humanities provenance: matching at work, section, and sen-tence granularity, in the source language, against an open-ended interpretive target. Recent LLM work on digital humanities (intertextuality (Yang et al., 2025), historical-text analysis (Zeng, 2024), heritage retrieval (Kelly et al., 2025), text reuse (McManus et al., 2025; Duan, 2025), and classi-cal translation and entity recognition (Chen et al., 2025a; Zhao et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2025), much of it at dedicated venues (Sprugnoli and Passarotti, 2024; Anderson et al., 2025; Hämäläinen et al., 2025)) each targets one task or corpus, not general citation-grounded assistance for primary-source re-search. 

2.3 Scholarly Primitives and close-reading theory 

The gaps above point back to humanities method-ology itself: scholarly practice already provides op-3erations for organizing interpretive work and close reading. Scholarly Primitives theory identifies com-mon operations in humanities research: discover-ing, annotating, comparing, referring, sampling, illustrating, and representing (Unsworth, 2000). Work on scholarly information practices and hu-manities visualization similarly treats humanistic work as recurring practices of information seek-ing, interpretation, organization, and representa-tion (Palmer et al., 2009; Drucker, 2011). We use Scholarly Primitives because they summarize broad humanistic practice into repeatable operations that can be translated into agent steps. Close-reading theory further explains the needed evidence substrate: interpretation does not only re-trieve relevant passages but situates them within textual, historical, and conceptual relations. Con-textual reading (Skinner and the Cambridge School) ties meaning to historically attested texts, authors, intentions, and arguments (Skinner, 1969); recurrent reading (Lovejoy’s history of ideas) fol-lows conceptual formations across periods, works, and traditions, including cases without direct tex-tual links (Lovejoy, 1936). Though contextualists warn against decontextualized histories of ideas (Skinner, 1969; Armitage, 2012), the two modes answer different evidentiary needs. SPIRE there-fore supports its Scholarly-Primitive agents with multi-scale close-reading retrieval: passages for di-rect quotation, graph communities for intra-context relational evidence, and semantic clusters for cross-context recurrent evidence. 

3 Methods: SPIRE 

3.1 Overview 

Building on the scholarly primitives and close-reading modes of §2.3, SPIRE turns each schol-arly primitive into an agent and each reading mode into a retrieval tier. A user supplies a classical-text corpus and an entity/relation schema, and SPIRE answers a research question with a evidence-grounded essay (Figure 1). Seven scholarly-primitive agents (§3.2) grow and curate a single shared EvidencePool from retrieval tiers, synthesize an argument, and re-ground it through reflection ( ≤ 2 rounds); they operate over a multi-scale close-reading infrastructure (§3.3) that com-piles the corpus bottom-up into a persistent store of text chunks, an entity-relation graph, intra-context communities, and cross-context clusters. 3.3 Multi-scale close-reading infrastructure 3.3.1 Corpora and preprocessing 

We instantiate SPIRE on two classical corpora cen-tral to humanities scholarship: classical Chinese (Zhongguo Xueshu Mingzhu Tiyao (Zhou, 1992)) and Greco-Roman Latin ( The Latin Library (The Latin Library, 2026)). History graduate students an-notate every work with metadata including author, date, alternative titles. Both span antiquity to the modern period and are standard primary sources in history, philology, and literary study (sizes and spans in Table 2). We segment each with tiktoken 

into 1000 -token chunks ( 100 -token overlap), giv-ing units U = {u1, . . . , u M }. Graph extraction and community/cluster reports use D EEP SEEK -V4-FLASH (thinking; temperature 0.1; App. G), while the answer-generation agents use the D EEP SEEK -V4-F LASH generator setting in §4.2; one mul-tilingual encoder, BGE-M3 (Chen et al., 2024a) (d=1024 , dense), embeds every object and query, so a single encoded query scores against every tier and matches Chinese and Latin without a per-tier or per-language encoder.

4.3 Metrics and judges Evidence retrieval. We use IR recall to test whether a system retrieves the same primary-source evidence cited in the reference paper. Gold evidence is considered recovered when its source work is retrieved and any of three signals fires: a verbatim/paper-context substring match, a section/chapter-locator match, or a BGE-M3 co-sine above 0.8 within-language ( 0.7 across, for translated quotations). We report recall at three provenance granularities: sentR (exact sentence), 

secR (cited section), and workR (source work); plus rank-independent eR over the full pool and MRR/nDCG (Järvelin and Kekäläinen, 2002) on 

secR . Recalls are macro-averaged across papers (%); Table 4 reports k=10 , the full k ∈ { 5, 10 , 20 }

Answer similarity. As a standard text-generation measure, we also report each essay’s surface simi-larity to the paper’s abstract and findings (BGE-M3 cosine, BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020), ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004); full numbers in App. A). 

Scholarly judge. Our primary arbiter is human: two humanities scholars (classics; philosophy) score each answer 1–5 on AnswerAccuracy, Argu-mentDepth, CoverageCompleteness, and Evidence-Quality (rubric in App. H), over a stratified sample of 100 papers ( 20 per category of Table 3). The protocol is free and comparative: raters see only the question and the four systems’ anonymised, shuffled answers, never the reference paper’s own answer. Two LLM raters (GPT-5.4, Q WEN 3.5-122B-A10B) replicate this as a scalable proxy (Liu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2026); they also run a reference track (additionally shown the paper’s findings), which preserves the ranking (App. H).

SPIRE leads the scholarly judge on every as-pect, for both LLM raters and both human experts (Table 5), and raters prefer it to each baseline on at least 96 of the 100 judged papers. The pat-tern is diagnostic. The no-retrieval Naive LLM stays on-question (AnswerAccuracy ≈3.9) yet can-not ground its claims (EvidenceQuality ≈2.1): it names works without quoting them. Text RAG and GraphRAG do retrieve, but quote tersely, only in the paper’s language, or refuse, scoring low on

Both experts also flagged that the Text RAG and GraphRAG baselines render primary sources only in the paper’s language, and that graph retrieval can reach a sec-ondary commentator rather than the primary text. 6 Conclusion 

We introduce SPIRE , a multi-agent framework for evidence-grounded scholarship in the classical hu-manities. It operationalizes Scholarly Primitives as the unit of computation through seven cooperating agents over a shared EvidencePool and a multi-scale substrate of passages, graph communities, and semantic clusters, making retrieval, contextual-ization, comparison, and writing inspectable. On a peer-reviewed-paper benchmark over clas-sical Chinese and Latin scholarship, SPIRE 

achieves higher primary-source evidence retrieval than Naive LLM, Text RAG, and GraphRAG, and 8receives higher human and LLM judge scores. Its contribution is an architecture making agen-tic humanities research assistance operational and inspectable, together with a peer-reviewed-paper benchmark and evaluation protocol for further hu-manities AI research. Future work will explore broader humanities sources and multimodal exten-sions. 

Limitations 

Instantiated traditions. We instantiate and eval-uate SPIRE on classical Chinese and Greco-Roman Latin corpora, two rich and demand-ing reservoirs of humanistic scholarship. These traditions provide strong testbeds for evidence-grounded reasoning because they require multilin-gual source recovery, close reading, commentary awareness, and cross-text comparison. Future work will extend the same framework to a broader range of humanities domains, including manuscript stud-ies, vernacular and modern literatures, religious studies, archival history, and scholarship in addi-tional languages and historiographic traditions. 

Textual evidence as foundation. This paper focuses on textual evidence—passages, works, chapters, quotations, and relations among texts— because such materials are foundational to classical studies, philology, philosophy, history, literature, and many adjacent fields. As humanities research increasingly brings together texts with images, ar-chaeological objects, performance records, maps, and archival materiality, an important next step is to extend SPIRE with multimodal retrieval, prove-nance tracking, and agent analysis over non-textual evidence while preserving the same emphasis on verifiable grounding. 

Human-centered scholarship. SPIRE is de-signed to assist humanistic inquiry by making source discovery, citation checking, comparison, and evidence-bound synthesis more inspectable and easier to teach. This is especially useful for students and early-stage researchers learning how to move from sources to arguments, and for experts who want a transparent assistant for exploring large corpora. Humanistic interpretation remains scholar-led: the framework is intended to support close reading, pedagogy, and peer review, while keeping final interpretive authority with researchers. 

Ethics Statement 

We evaluate AI assistance for humanities research rather than replacement of humanities researchers. 

SPIRE supports evidence discovery, citation checking, and interpretive drafting in a provenance-aware workflow, with the goal of widening access to rigorous source-based scholarship and helping students, early-stage researchers, and experts in-spect the evidentiary path from primary texts to arguments. The framework should be used as a human-centered research assistant: it strengthens documentation and review, while scholarly judge-ment, teaching responsibility, and peer evaluation remain with human researchers. 

Artifact use and data protection. The released artifacts are intended for non-commercial research and evaluation of evidence-grounded humanities research assistants. They should not be used to re-place scholarly judgment, generate uncited claims, or redistribute restricted source materials outside their original access conditions. The benchmark is built from published scholarship and classical primary sources rather than private personal data. Author identities from paper-derived records are not used as model inputs or evaluation targets, and released benchmark records are anonymized or re-stricted according to source access conditions. 

Human annotation and evaluation. Annotators and expert raters were recruited through academic collaboration rather than crowdsourcing. They were graduate students or scholars with relevant ex-pertise in history, philology, classics, or philosophy. They were compensated or credited according to local institutional practice, and no demographic in-formation was collected. All annotators and raters were informed that their annotations and ratings would be used for research evaluation and reported only in aggregate or anonymized form. 

Code and Data Release 

Code, data store, and benchmark are released at 

https://github.com/YatingPan/SPIRE . We re-lease the code under the MIT License and the benchmark metadata under CC BY-NC 4.0 where redistribution is permitted. The source texts are un-der copyright and we cannot redistribute them, so the repository instead ships the catalogues needed to obtain legally licensed copies, together with our derived, non-infringing artefacts. The Chinese cor-pus is built from Zhongguo Xueshu Mingzhu Tiyao 

9(Compendium of Chinese Academic Masterworks ,Fudan University Press) (Zhou, 1992); the Latin corpus from The Latin Library (The Latin Library, 2026). For both, we release a plain-text bibliog-raphy (the work list) so that others can locate and download legally licensed editions, alongside the extracted graph and cluster data in JSONL (no raw full text). For the peer-reviewed-paper benchmark (humanities articles that are typically absent from open preprint servers and often require purchase or institutional access), we likewise provide the paper list and the extracted benchmark (research questions, findings, and cited primary-source ev-idence, in JSON) in JSON rather than the PDFs. Source texts and paper-derived records are used and distributed only according to their original ac-cess conditions; scripts are provided to reconstruct restricted components when redistribution is not allowed. All released data is limited to scholarly metadata, structured annotations, and derived rep-resentations needed for reproducibility, and avoids personal or sensitive information.

A.1 Evidence-recall matching rule 

A gold evidence is recovered when its source work is retrieved and a verbatim or paper-context sub-string matches (after NFKC normalization and punctuation stripping), a chapter locator matches, or the BGE-M3 cosine clears a threshold ( τs=0 .80 

same-language, τc=0 .70 cross-language, since some papers quote their sources in translation); aggregation is paper-level macro-mean. Sensitivity to τs, τ c is reported in §A.4.

A.4 Threshold sensitivity 

The evidence matcher in the main results uses 

(τs, τ c) = (0 .80 , 0.70) (§4.3; §A), fixed a priori 

as conventional BGE-M3 semantic-match oper-ating points; they were not tuned on a labelled development split. We deliberately avoid fitting the matcher to our own benchmark, because these thresholds govern the evaluation (when a retrieved passage counts as recovering a gold evidence) rather than any system’s behaviour. The headline scores are reported in Table 4 and Table 7. As an additional diagnostic audit of threshold robust-ness, Table 9 sweeps both thresholds from very permissive (0 .70 , 0.60) to very strict (0 .90 , 0.80) 

for SPIRE (full ), and Table 10 reports the same diagnostic full-pool evidence recall (eR) compari-son for every system. Three patterns hold across the sweep. (i) Work-level recall is invariant ( workR@10 = 51 .2 at ev-ery setting), because it depends only on whether a gold evidence’s source work enters the top-k, not on sentence-level cosine. (ii) Sentence recall is the most threshold-sensitive metric ( sentR@10 falls from 18 .2 to 12 .2), being the signal most reliant on semantic matching; section recall moves less (23 .8 → 21 .8) because it is also supported by loca-tor and chapter matches, and aggregate evidence recall declines smoothly ( 57 .9 → 46 .5) as semantic-only matches are filtered out. (iii) Most importantly, the ranking of systems is stable at every threshold :

SPIRE leads on evidence recall under all five set-tings (Table 10), by ∼18 points over the strongest baseline. The main-paper setting (0 .80 , 0.70) sits in the middle of this range, so it neither flatters nor penalises any system relative to the alternatives. These tables are a diagnostic threshold audit rather than a replacement for the headline retrieval table; Table 4 and Table 7 remain the source of the main reported scores. 

A.5 Inter-rater agreement: AC2 vs. weighted 

κ

We report two complementary chance-corrected agreement coefficients on the free -track scores, both with quadratic (ordinal) weights: quadratic-weighted Cohen’s κ and Gwet’s AC2 (Gwet, 2008). They differ only in how they model “chance,” and the gap between them is itself informative, so we re-port both (Table 12, Fig. 3) and treat the prevalence-robust AC2 as primary. 

Two notions of chance. κ corrects against the agreement expected if each rater drew scores in-dependently from their own marginal; AC2 cor-rects against a baseline that does not assume such independence. When scores concentrate at one end of the scale—as here, since every rater scores

Humanities citation norms expect the canonical published translation (e.g. the Loeb Classical Li-brary edition) for such passages—accurate, vetted, and citable—whereas an ad hoc model translation is none of these. Pairing the retrieved primary text with a registered translation resource would resolve this. 

GraphRAG: secondary literature substituted for the primary text (severe). On H008 (Dao-ism, Confucianism, and Augustine on learning), asked about the Daoist position, GraphRAG never reaches Laozi or Zhuangzi; it instead retrieves a Neo-Confucian critic and a Qing philologist, and imports Buddhism the question never raised: 

> The retrieved passage from Zhang Zai criticizes Buddhism and Daoism. . . The passage from Dai Zhen notes that Laozi said “abandon learning and be free of worry”. . .

Expert B scored EvidenceQuality = 1 and noted that the Daoist side used neither Zhuangzi nor direct Daoist evidence, but secondary literature from Zhang Zai and even introduced Buddhism. Community-summary retrieval surfaces commen-tary about the topic rather than the cited primary source. 

Text RAG: off-topic retrieval, then refusal (se-vere). On CROSS_006 (“should Confucius be re-garded as a Sinicus Cicero ?”), flat cosine retrieval returns only unrelated Latin rhetoric (Quintilian’s 

Institutio Oratoria , Seneca’s De Brevitate Vitae and 

Epistulae )—nothing on Confucius or the Confucius Sinarum Philosophus —so the answer collapses to a single sentence: 

> “The retrieved passages do not contain any infor-mation about Confucius . . . Therefore, they are insufficient to answer whether Confucius should be regarded as a Sinicus Cicero.”

When retrieval misses, the flat baseline neither sup-plements nor reasons; its answers collapse to such refusals (median length ∼270 characters), which the judge penalises on every axis. 

Naive LLM: ungrounded recall (severe). With no retrieval, the no-retrieval baseline answers CROSS_001 (Confucius vs. Cicero) fluently from parametric memory and cites from recollection:     

> {source: Lunyu / Analects 12.1, “to master oneself and return to ritual is ren ”; role: central; confi-dence: high}; {source: De officiis . . . }

The named loci are plausible but unverifiable against the corpus and prone to misattribution— the very failure that motivates evidence-grounded retrieval. In sum, SPIRE ’s residual errors presuppose it found and quoted the right primary sources (it then mis-periodises a term, or self-translates), whereas the baselines fail upstream—retrieving wrong or secondary material, or nothing usable. This is why the gap is largest precisely on EvidenceQuality (Table 5) and on work/section recall (Table 4).

27 Fragment 3: Reconstructing the positive case 

Dimension: balanced coverage 

> Naive LLM

Quintus relies on Stoic sympatheia and divine providence as explanatory frameworks, claim-ing that the gods communicate through signs that trained interpreters can read.

. . . . . . This comparison is crucial because it frames divination as a species of empiri-cal knowledge: it is opinabilis , a matter of conjecture, but conjecture disciplined by experience. 

Analysis. SPIRE first reconstructs Quintus’ strongest case: divination as an empirical and con-jectural art. That balanced reconstruction makes the later critique sharper because Marcus is shown to challenge a plausible evidential practice rather than a caricature.
