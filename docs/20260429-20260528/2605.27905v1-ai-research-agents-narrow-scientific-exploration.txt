# AI Research Agents Narrow Scientific Exploration 

Yixuan Tang, Yi Yang 

The Hong Kong University of Science and Technology 

ytangch@connect.ust.hk, imyiyang@ust.hk 

## Abstract 

AI research agents can now generate research ideas, design experiments, run code, and draft papers, raising the possibility of large-scale AI-assisted scientific discovery. Many current agent frameworks explicitly encourage the generation of novel and high-impact ideas. Yet it remains unclear whether AI-assisted ideation broadens scientific exploration or mainly concentrates around existing work. We study AI research agents as scientific search systems. Using four AI research-agent frameworks and six large language models, we generate 37,802 scientific ideas from shared seed literature across citation-defined research areas in AI and machine learning. We then compare the resulting AI ideas against human-authored papers from the same research areas, follow-on human research emerging from the same seed literature, and the seed literature itself. Across experiments, four consistent patterns emerge. First, AI-generated ideas are substantially more concentrated than human-authored papers from the same research areas. Second, AI-generated ideas remain much closer to their starting literature than later human follow-on work does. Third, papers most similar to AI-generated ideas tend to receive lower subsequent citations. Fourth, when AI-generated ideas differ from prior work, the differences arise primarily from recombining existing technical methods rather than introducing fundamentally new research questions. Overall, current AI research agents appear better suited to local elaboration than to broadening scientific exploration. 

## Introduction 

Recent advances in AI research agents have raised the possibility of automating scientific discovery. These agents can now generate research ideas, plan experiments, run code, write papers, and iteratively explore and refine scientific hypotheses (Lu et al., 2026; Baek et al., 2025; Schmidgall et al., 2025). Importantly, these AI research agent frameworks are explicitly designed to encourage exploratory scientific ideation. Their prompts and reasoning procedures often instruct agents to generate novel, high-impact, and unconventional ideas rather than simple extensions of prior workLu et al. (2026); Baek et al. (2025); Schmidgall et al. (2025). As such systems become increasingly capable and accessible, they may fundamentally reshape the process of scientific discovery. Yet the ability to generate research ideas at scale does not necessarily imply broader scientific exploration. Scientific discovery often depends on moving beyond established directions, searching less familiar regions, and recombining prior knowledge in non-routine ways (Foster et al., 2015; Uzzi et al., 2013). Existing evaluations of AI research agents mainly assess whether individual ideas are interesting, novel, feasible, or executable (Si et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2024b), but reveal much less about how repeated AI-assisted ideation may shape the broader landscape of scientific exploration. This raises a broader question: do AI research agents broaden scientific exploration, or mainly stay close to existing work? To study this question, we construct citation-defined research areas from the AI and machine learning literature and repeatedly use AI research agents to generate ideas from shared seed papers within each

In total, our experiments generate 37,802 AI-generated research ideas. Throughout the ideation process, these agent frameworks are explicitly prompted to explore novel research directions beyond the seed literature. We then investigate these AI-generated ideas from four perspectives: whether they explore diverse scientific directions, whether they move beyond their starting literature toward new research ar-eas, whether they are associated with potentially high-impact regions of the scientific landscape, and whether their differences from prior work arise mainly from new research questions or from recombinations of existing technical methods. Across the generated ideas, four consistent patterns emerge. First, AI-generated ideas are substantially more concentrated than human-authored papers from the same research area. Compared with human research, AI-generated ideas explore a less diverse range of scientific directions. Second, AI-generated ideas remain much closer to their starting literature than later human follow-on work does. Rather than moving substantially beyond the seed literature, AI-generated ideas largely stay within the conceptual neighborhood of the input seed papers. Third, AI-generated ideas tend to occupy lower-impact regions of the scientific landscape. Finally, when AI-generated ideas differ from prior work, the differences arise primarily from recombining existing technical methods rather than introducing fundamentally new research questions. These findings suggest a distinction between generating plausible research ideas and broadening scientific exploration. Current AI agents can efficiently produce coherent, literature-grounded proposals at scale, but their outputs largely remain near existing directions of prior work. As AI systems become more deeply integrated into scientific workflows, this distinction may become increasingly important. The broader challenge is therefore not only how to make AI systems generate more plausible scientific ideas, but how to design systems that expand the range of scientific exploration itself.

Scientific Idea generation. We next use AI research agents to generate new scientific ideas from prior literature. For each citation-defined research area, we repeatedly sample sets of seed papers from the 2022–2025 analysis pool to initialize AI-guided idea generation. Each seed set contains five papers: one anchor paper together with four related papers from the same research area, selected using citation and lexical similarity. We use five seed papers because most evaluated AI research agent frameworks are constrained by context-window limitations of current LLMs. Using five papers provides sufficient literature context for ideation. AI agents then explore the literature context and generate new research ideas conditioned on these seed papers. We repeatedly bootstrap different seed-paper sets from the same research area, producing distinct starting contexts for AI-guided scientific ideation. We evaluate four representative AI research-agent frameworks: a zero-shot baseline, AIScientist (Lu et al., 2026), ResearchAgent (Baek et al., 2025), and AgentLaboratory (Schmidgall et al., 2025). These frameworks represents several major designs for AI research agent, including iterative self-reflection, multi-stage planning and validation, and multi-agent deliberation. Table 1 summarizes the evaluated frameworks and their corresponding agentic mechanisms. Importantly, across all evaluated frameworks, the ideation prompts explicitly encourage exploration beyond the seed literature. The zero-shot baseline asks the model to propose a novel research idea. AIScientist emphasizes generating “novel” and “high-impact” ideas through iterative self-reflection and revision. ResearchAgent encourages innovative method design during multi-stage planning and validation. Agent Laboratory explicitly instructs agents to expand upon the literature and generate ideas that are “very innovative and unlike anything seen before.” Appendix B provides the full prompts and detailed agentic design of each evaluated framework. Each AI agent framework needs to be paired with an LLM. We evaluate six LLMs spanning three model families (Qwen, Llama, and Gemma) and scales ranging from 0.8B to 35B parameters: Qwen3.5-0.8B (Qwen Team, 2026), Qwen3.5-4B (Qwen Team, 2026), Qwen3.6-35B-A3B (Qwen Team, 2026), Llama-3.2-1B (Grattafiori et al., 2024), Llama-3.1-8B (Grattafiori et al., 2024), and Gemma-4-31B-IT (Farabet and Lacombe, 2026). 3For each combination of four AI research-agent frameworks and six LLMs, we repeatedly bootstrap seed-paper sets 2,140 times across the citation-defined research areas. In total, we collect 51,360 generation runs and retain 37,802 valid generated ideas, where validity requires successful completion with non-empty structured output. All analyses are conducted using valid ideas. Appendix B provides the detailed breakdown and examples of AI-generated scientific ideas. To enable consistent comparison across different agent outputs, in the downstream analysis, we further represent each AI-generated research idea and each human-authored seed paper using two core elements: what problem the work studies and how it proposes to solve that problem. Scientific novelty is often associated with new research questions, new technical methods, or new combinations of existing ideas (Luo et al., 2022; Uzzi et al., 2013). We therefore prompt an LLM to decompose each generated idea into a research question , describing the problem being studied, and technical methods , describing how the idea approaches that problem. Appendix C describes the extraction procedure. 

## Quantifying AI-Generated Scientific Ideas 

We next introduce several measurements to characterize AI-generated scientific ideas. First, we ask whether AI-generated ideas are as diverse as human-authored research within the same scientific area. Second, we ask whether AI-generated ideas move toward new research directions or remain close to existing literature. Third, we ask whether AI-generated ideas are associated with higher downstream scientific impact, measured through citation patterns of semantically similar human papers. Finally, we examine how AI-generated papers differ from the seed papers. 

Exploration breadth. We first measure the diversity of AI-generated ideas within the same citation-defined research area (Peng et al., 2021). To quantify exploration breadth, we embed both AI-generated ideas and human-authored papers into a shared semantic space and compute pairwise cosine similarity between papers from the same research area. Higher within-area similarity indi-cates that generated ideas occupy a narrower region of the idea space and explore fewer distinct directions (Hofstra et al., 2020; Cohan et al., 2020). 

Distance from prior literature. We next examine how far AI-generated ideas move away from the seed literature used to initialize ideation. For each bootstrap-generated seed-paper set, we compute the embedding similarity between AI-generated ideas and the corresponding seed papers. We then compare this distance against that of follow-on human-authored papers connected to the same seed literature. This measurement quantifies whether AI-generated ideas remain locally anchored to prior work or move toward newer research directions (Shibayama et al., 2021). 

Potential scientific impact. To estimate the potential impact of AI-generated ideas, we identify later human-authored papers that are semantically similar to the generated ideas. We then analyze the citation statistics of these matched human papers (Arts et al., 2025). This provides an exploratory measurement of whether regions close to AI-generated ideas are associated with higher downstream scientific impact. 

New research questions, new methods, or recombination. Finally, we examine how AI-generated ideas differ from the seed literature used to initialize ideation. Note that each AI-generated idea is produced from a bootstrap sample of five seed papers drawn from the same citation-defined research area. We thus compare the extracted research questions and technical methods in each AI-generated idea against the corresponding research questions and technical methods appearing in those five seed papers. If a research question or technical method already appears in the seed literature, we treat it as grounded in prior work. Otherwise, we treat it as newly introduced relative to the seed literature. This comparison allows us to examine whether AI-generated ideas primarily differ from prior work through new research questions, new technical methods, or recombinations of existing methods (Luo et al., 2022; Uzzi et al., 2013).

AI Ideas Stay Close to Their Starting Literature 

The previous section shows that AI-generated ideas occupy a substantially narrower region of the scientific idea space. We next examine whether these generated ideas move beyond the seed literature used to initialize ideation or remain locally anchored to it. For each bootstrap-generated seed-paper set, we identify later human-authored papers that cite at least two of the seed papers. These follow-on papers represent subsequent human research emerging from the same starting literature. We then compare how far AI-generated ideas and follow-on human papers move away from the same seed literature. Specifically, we measure the cosine similarity between AI-generated ideas and the seed papers, as well as the similarity between follow-on human papers and those same seed papers. Figure 3 shows a consistent pattern across all agent frameworks and LLMs. AI-generated ideas remain substantially closer to the seed literature (0.92) than later human follow-on papers do (0.88). Moreover, similarity between AI-generated ideas and follow-on human papers is lowest (0.82). This indicates that AI-generated ideas and later human research often evolve toward different directions. Figure 4 visualizes this pattern for representative examples. AI-generated ideas form concentrated clusters near the seed papers, while later human papers are more dispersed and often move farther away from the original literature context. Together, these results suggest that current AI research agents mainly extrapolate locally from prior literature rather than substantially moving beyond it. 6AI Ideas are Located in Lower-Impact Regions of the Scientific Landscape 

We next examine whether AI-generated ideas tend to occupy influential regions of the scientific landscape. Because AI-generated ideas themselves do not have real-world citation outcomes, we instead identify human-authored papers that are semantically similar to the generated ideas and use their citation patterns as a proxy for downstream scientific impact. To study this question, we match AI-generated ideas with human-authored papers whose textual embeddings have cosine similarity above 0.9 (see Appendix E for representative examples). For each matched paper, we compare its citation count with the average citation count of papers from the same publication year and citation-defined research area. Across 2,359 matched idea–paper pairs, papers most similar to AI-generated ideas receive 50.4 citations on average, compared with 54.9 citations for the same-area baseline. The mean difference is -4.47 citations (95% CI: [-6.41, -2.53], p < 0.001 ). This below-baseline pattern holds for three of the four agent frameworks (Table 2). These results suggest that AI-generated ideas tend to occupy relatively lower-impact regions of the scientific landscape. Although current AI agents can generate coherent and plausible research ideas, the surrounding scientific directions appear more locally incremental than highly influential. AI Ideas Mainly Differ Through Method Recombination Rather Than New Research Questions 

We next examine how AI-generated ideas differ from the seed literature used to initialize ideation. For each AI-generated idea, we compare its extracted research question and technical methods against the corresponding research questions and technical methods appearing in the five seed papers used during ideation. A generated research question or technical method is treated as already present when its cosine similarity to the closest seed-literature counterpart exceeds 0.87. This threshold is manually calibrated through inspection of representative semantic matches and mismatches. Appendix F provides examples used to calibrate this threshold. The analysis reveals a clear asymmetry between research questions and technical methods. AI-generated ideas rarely introduce substantially new research questions: in 85.1% of AI-generated ideas, the research question already appears in the seed literature (Figure 5). In contrast, technical methods exhibit substantially greater variation. Only 62.6% of AI-generated ideas contain technical methods already appearing in the seed literature. Importantly, this qualitative pattern is not particularly sensitive to the exact similarity threshold. Because both research questions and technical methods are evaluated under the same matching criterion, AI-generated ideas consistently show substantially more variation at the method level than at the research-question level across threshold choices. This pattern remains consistent across agent frameworks. AIScientist produces the greatest amount of method variation, whereas ResearchAgent remains the most conservative. However, the share of generated ideas containing no new research question remains high across all frameworks, ranging from 79.0% for AIScientist to 88.4% for ResearchAgent. Overall, these findings suggest that current AI research agents more often modify and recombine technical methods within existing research questions than generate fundamentally new research questions. 7Zero-shot

## Discussion and Implications for AI-Assisted Scientific Discovery 

Our results suggest a more cautious interpretation of current AI research agents. Recent systems introduce increasingly sophisticated agentic mechanisms into scientific ideation, including iterative self-reflection, staged validation, role decomposition, and multi-agent deliberation (Lu et al., 2026; Baek et al., 2025; Schmidgall et al., 2025). These mechanisms can improve the coherence and plausibility of generated research proposals. However, our findings suggest that such capabilities do not necessarily translate into broader scientific exploration. Although these AI agents are specifically asked to propose novel, high-impact, or unlike-prior-work ideas, AI-generated ideas remain substan-tially more concentrated than human-authored research, stay closer to the starting literature than later human follow-on work, and primarily arise from recombinations of existing technical methods rather than fundamentally new research questions. This distinction matters because scientific discovery is not only about producing plausible ideas, but also about exploring the space of possible ideas. Human scientific progress often involves moving beyond established directions, exploring less familiar regions, and occasionally reframing the underlying research problem itself (Foster et al., 2015; Uzzi et al., 2013; Fortunato et al., 2018). From this perspective, current AI research agents appear better suited to local elaboration than exploration. They efficiently recombine and refine ideas around existing conceptual neighborhoods, but less frequently move toward the more dispersed directions later explored by human researchers. Our findings also suggest that scaling and increasingly sophisticated agentic AI frameworks do not fundamentally resolve this limitation. The observed patterns remain remarkably stable across different model families, parameter scales, and agent architectures. Multi-agent discussion, iterative reflection, and staged validation may improve proposal quality without substantially broadening the explored scientific landscape. More broadly, our findings point toward a future challenge for AI-guided scientific discovery. The central question may not only be whether AI systems can generate coherent scientific ideas, but whether they can help expand the range of scientific directions under consideration. As AI research agents become more deeply integrated into scientific workflows, designing agentic AI systems that support exploratory breadth may become increasingly important.

## A Data Sources and Research Area Construction 

Overview. We identify research areas using bibliographic coupling. The main idea is that papers studying related problems tend to cite the same prior work. We therefore represent each paper by the references it cites, transform these high-dimensional reference profiles into compact paper embeddings, and cluster papers with similar embeddings. 10 Corpus and metadata. We collect papers and associated metadata from DBLP for ICLR, NeurIPS, and ICML, three leading AI/ML conferences. For each paper, the metadata include its title, authors, abstract, keywords, and cited references. The resulting corpus contains 34,698 papers published between 2019 and 2025 (Table 3). We use papers published between 2022–2025 as the primary analysis pool, denoted by P. Papers published between 2019–2021 provide additional citation information linking papers within P, which is used to construct the citation graph underlying bibliographic coupling. Table 3: Statistics for ICLR, NeurIPS, and ICML papers after adding DBLP citation records. Year 2019 2020 2021 2022 2023 2024 2025 Total Papers 2,416 3,298 4,123 4,820 6,621 9,250 4,170 34,698 

Reference-based paper representation. We construct research areas from the 24,861 papers published in 2022–2025. Let R denote the set of papers in the 2019–2025 corpus that can be cited as references. For each paper p ∈ P , let Rp ⊆ R be the set of corpus papers cited by p.Bibliographic coupling measures paper relatedness based on shared references (Kessler, 1963). Two papers are considered related when they cite similar prior literature. Under a matrix formulation, if B ∈ { 0, 1}|P|×|R| denotes the paper–reference matrix, then the standard bibliographic-coupling matrix is given by 

C = BB ⊤,

where each entry counts the number of references jointly cited by two papers. The raw coupling matrix C captures shared citation structure, but it treats all references equally. In practice, some highly cited papers (e.g., widely used optimization methods or foundation architectures) are cited across many unrelated research areas and therefore provide relatively weak evidence of topical similarity. Therefore, we construct paper embeddings directly from the paper–reference matrix B. To reduce the influence of broadly cited papers, we apply an inverse-document-frequency-style weighting scheme to the columns of B, downweighting references that are frequently cited across the corpus and assigning greater importance to more topic-specific references. The weighted rows are L2-normalized and projected to d = 128 dimensions using truncated SVD, yielding dense paper embeddings 

X ∈ R24 ,861 ×128 .

Research area identification. Research areas are identified by clustering the embeddings of papers published in 2022–2025. HDBSCAN (McInnes et al., 2017) is applied with min_cluster_size = 15 

and min_samples = 5 , producing 148 non-noise clusters that together contain 13,126 assigned papers. One large heterogeneous cluster (6,462 papers) with diffuse reference overlap is removed, leaving 147 candidate research areas containing 6,664 assigned papers. Only clusters that contain papers from every year in 2022–2025 are retained. This longitudinal filter yields 19 research areas with 1,035 papers in total. Figure 6 shows the per-year distribution of papers across these research areas, and Figure 7 visualizes them in the embedding space.

## D Alternative Centroid-Based Measure of Exploration Breadth 

As a robustness check for the pairwise-similarity analysis, we measure exploration breadth using centroid distances. For each citation-defined research area, we compute the normalized centroid of the embedding distribution and measure the cosine distance between each paper and its corresponding area centroid. Lower distances indicate that papers are more tightly concentrated around the center of the same-area distribution. Table 7 reports the resulting centroid-distance statistics across AI agent frameworks and LLMs. Consistent with the main pairwise-similarity analysis, AI-generated ideas remain substantially more concentrated than human-authored papers across all evaluated settings.

## F Threshold Calibration for Semantic Matching 

We choose the cosine-similarity threshold of 0.87 by manually inspecting pairs of generated research-question or technical-method items and seed-literature items across a range of similarity scores. Table 9 shows examples just above and below the threshold. Pairs above 0.87 capture the same underlying research question or technical method despite surface differences (e.g., “risk-aware” vs. “risk-averse” reinforcement learning). Pairs below 0.87 reflect different research-question or 22 technical-method items (e.g., “adaptive label smoothing” is a different technical method rather than a synonym for “label smoothing”). Importantly, the findings are not particularly sensitive to the exact threshold choice. Because both research questions and technical methods are evaluated under the same matching criterion, AI-generated ideas consistently exhibit substantially greater variation at the technical-method level than at the research-question level across a range of reasonable thresholds.
