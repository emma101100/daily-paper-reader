# What Makes a Medical Checker Trainable? Diagnosing Signal Collapse and Reward Hacking in Checker-Guided RAG for Biomedical QA 

Yuelyu Ji Min Gu Kwak Hang Zhang Xizhi Wu Chenyu Li Yanshan Wang 

University of Pittsburgh, Pittsburgh, PA, USA 

yueluji@gmail.com 

Abstract 

Medical RAG needs evidence-grounded claims, so plugging a claim-level NLI checker into retrieval-augmented RL is intuitive. We find that the checker’s output distribution dur-ing training, not its held-out accuracy, de-cides whether it provides trainable gradient. 

We compare four NLI checker back-ends as process rewards inside a GRPO-trained med-ical RAG agent (Qwen2.5-7B, replicated on Qwen3-4B and Llama-3.1-8B) across four held-out medical QA benchmarks. Three diagnos-tic findings emerge. (i) Signal collapse is log-prob-specific: LLM log-probability scoring la-bels over 97% of claims neutral—collapsing the RL gradient to zero—while a calibrated MedNLI classifier scores the same pairs non-degenerately. (ii) Moderate signal beats strong signal on answer quality: a strong proprietary checker triggers a three-step reward-hacking cascade—ultra-short answers, search avoid-ance, language collapse—so a moderate-signal local classifier trains a higher-quality model (+12% BERTScore over zero-shot, no GPT dependency ). (iii) Signal strength is policy-dependent: the same checker registers as mod-erate on one policy but strong on another with-out triggering the cascade end-state. We frame these as boundary conditions for verifier-as-reward systems.

The intu-itive design is to plug a claim-level NLI checker into the RL reward path, so that the policy learns to generate evidence-grounded claims through retrieval-augmented RL (Jin et al., 2025; Xiong et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2025). This design is be-ing explored in concurrent medical RAG-RL sys-tems (Tamo et al., 2026; Xu et al., 2025; Song et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2026, 2025; Liu et al., 2026, 2025b), and builds on a

broader literature where claim-level verification is used for evaluation and post-hoc correction (Min et al., 2023; Vladika et al., 2025a,b), with the headline metric being NLI accuracy on held-out pairs. However, no prior work treats the checker’s 

output distribution under the policy’s own roll-outs —as opposed to its accuracy on a held-out NLI benchmark—as a measurable training-time variable, and we will show that this distinction is where the design fails. In medical RAG, plugging an off-the-shelf NLI checker into the reward path fails in two ways that accuracy does not predict. A biomedical LLM scor-ing entailment by log-probability (Sallinen et al., 2025) can label >97% of claims N EUTRAL re-gardless of evidence (collapsing the per-claim sup-port rate, the fraction labelled E NTAIL , to zero gradient), even though the same model is accurate on balanced NLI tests. A strong proprietary ver-ifier (Achiam et al., 2023) that does not collapse instead opens a reward-hacking pathway (Skalse et al., 2022): ultra-short answers, then search avoid-ance, then generating non-English answers to evade the English-only NLI prompt; each shortcut closes under a countermeasure but exposes the next. Both failure modes are invisible to held-out NLI accuracy. Our central thesis is that the checker’s output distribution under the policy’s rollouts, not its held-out accuracy, decides whether it provides trainable gradient. We therefore fo-cus on the per-claim support rate—the fraction of generated claims the checker labels E NTAIL —as the primary diagnostic variable throughout the pa-per. This paper is a diagnosis of the checker-as-reward design space, not a competing system; we make no SOTA claim. We build a multi-turn medical RAG agent on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Hui et al., 2024) trained with GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), extending Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) with a dense retriever over the MedRAG corpus (Xiong et al., 2024) and a claim-level NLI verification server as process reward. To isolate how the checker’s output distribution shapes training, we instantiate four NLI back-ends as drop-in replacements inside the same reward pathway: Likelihood-NLI (Meditron-3-8B log-prob), MedNLI-Cls (PubMedBERT classifier), 

Hybrid (GPT atomic extraction + local log-prob scoring), and GPT-NLI (GPT-4o-mini end-to-end). The four span the ( extractor , scorer ) design space along a GPT-dependency gradient (Figure 1, Ta-ble 2); the per-claim mechanics are deferred to §3. We describe two operating regimes of per-claim support rate during training: moderate (≲60%, e.g. MedNLI-Cls at 54%) and strong (≳75%, e.g. Hy-brid at 76%, GPT-NLI at 86%). 1 We report three diagnostic findings. First , signal collapse is spe-cific to log-probability scoring: MedNLI-Cls pro-duces non-degenerate verdicts on the same pairs Likelihood-NLI labels almost entirely N EUTRAL 

(§5). Second , the relation between signal strength and answer quality is non-monotonic: a moderate-signal classifier outperforms a strong-signal pro-prietary checker (BERTScore 0.600 vs. 0.591) be-cause the latter triggers a three-step cascade of shortcuts (ultra-short answers, search avoidance, language collapse) that each undermine clinical groundedness (§6). Third , signal strength is a prop-erty of the policy–checker pair, not of the checker alone; we support this with one cross-model swap (Qwen2.5-7B → Qwen3-4B) and treat it as a work-ing hypothesis to be tested at larger scale (§ ?? ,App. 25). We frame these findings as boundary conditions for concurrent verifier-as-reward sys-tems. 

2 Related Work 

We organise prior and concurrent work along four axes: process rewards in retrieval-augmented RL, claim-level verification for biomedical RAG, NLI as a reward model, and failure modes in LLM RL. Table 1 summarises five concurrent verifier-as-reward systems on seven design dimensions and locates our contribution in that design space. 

Process rewards in retrieval-augmented RL. 

Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) trains LLMs to in-terleave reasoning and search through RL with outcome-based rewards on general-domain QA, using F1 alone as the reward signal. Concurrent verifier-as-reward systems extend this paradigm to medicine: EvidenceRL (Tamo et al., 2026) pairs PubMedBERT-MNLI / MedNLI with GRPO and reports a grounding-style reward; VERITAS (Xu et al., 2025) integrates faithfulness rewards into Search-R1 training and notes that NLI judgements in RAG settings can be unreliable; Med-R3 (Lu et al., 2025) uses progressive training with retrieval-effectiveness rewards; Fleming-R1 (Liu et al., 2025a) combines curriculum learning with RL for medical reasoning; and MedResearcher (Yu et al., 2025) trains agents for multi-hop medical evidence gathering. Claim-level verification for biomedical RAG. 

FActScore (Min et al., 2023) introduces atomic factuality evaluation by decomposing generations into verifiable units; RAGChecker (Ru et al., 2024) provides fine-grained diagnostic metrics including faithfulness, hallucination rate, and noise sensi-tivity; Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2023) trains reflec-tion tokens for adaptive retrieval and self-critique at generation time. Medical extensions include MedRAGChecker (Ji et al., 2026), which extends atomic factuality to biomedical RAG with claim-level verification; the atomic-fact decomposition for medical answers of Vladika et al. (2025a); and the iterative step-by-step verification system of Vladika et al. (2025b). The MIRAGE bench-mark and MedRAG toolkit (Xiong et al., 2024) and Med-PaLM 2 (Singhal et al., 2025) estab-lish the medical-RAG evaluation backdrop. These works use verification for evaluation or generation-time guidance; we study a substantially different setting—using verification as an RL reward signal during training—where systematic checker biases directly corrupt the training objective rather than merely producing noisy evaluation. 

NLI as a reward model. NLI is the backbone of most claim-verification systems. In the medical do-main, MedNLI (Romanov and Shivade, 2018) pro-vides a clinical NLI benchmark, and biomedical en-coders such as PubMedBERT (Gu et al., 2021) and SciFive (Phan et al., 2021) achieve strong perfor-mance on biomedical NLI; FActScore (Min et al., 2023) popularised NLI-style atomic verification at evaluation time. We provide, to our knowledge, the first systematic comparison of how different NLI architectures behave as process rewards inside an RL loop. 

Failure modes in LLM RL. Reward hacking is well-documented in general RL (Skalse et al., 2022). In LLM RL, concurrent work on Search-R1 variants reports that F1-based rewards cause training collapse through answer avoidance, and VERITAS (Xu et al., 2025) reports unreliable NLI judgements in RAG settings. We extend this litera-ture in two ways. We localise NLI failure to LLM log-probability scoring specifically and show that calibrated MedNLI-Cls classifiers do not collapse on the same claim–evidence pairs (§5); and we document a three-step reward-hacking cascade— ultra-short answers, search avoidance, language collapse—that emerges only under strong verifi-cation signal (§6). Together with Table 1, this positions our contribution as a diagnosis of when verifier-as-reward designs train, not a competing system.

4 Experiments 

4.1 Setup Model and training. All experiments fine-tune Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct with GRPO on 2 ×H100 GPUs ( ∼2 h per run); per-run training-step counts and full hyperparameters (batch size, learning rate, KL and entropy coefficients) are in Appendix 23. 

Datasets. Training combines CSIRO MedRedQA (Nguyen et al., 2023), LiveQA-Med (Liu et al., 2020), and PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019) into 1,513 train / 87 in-domain test (“Medical”). Held-out evaluation adds four out-of-distribution sets: MedicationQA (674, full standard test set, drug safety) (Liu et al., 2020), BioASQ-Y/N (618, MIRAGE 2019–2023 split, biomedical literature) (Tsatsaronis et al., 2015), MedQuAD (100, NIH consumer health subsam-ple) (Ben Abacha and Demner-Fushman, 2019), and MEDIQA (85, clinical summarisation) (Xun et al., 2025); total evaluation coverage is 1,564 samples. The closest non-RL comparison points on these datasets are MedBioLM (Kim, 2025a) and MedBioRAG (Kim, 2025b), both based on fine-tuned GPT-4o; we compare against them on MedicationQA (Table 4). 

Metrics. For answer quality we report BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019) (primary, seman-tic similarity to physician-authored references), token F1 (Rajpurkar et al., 2016), and ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004). Model outputs run 200–400 characters and references exceed 500, so F1 is systematically low across all configurations (zero-shot baseline F1 = 0.191), which differs from accuracy on closed-form benchmarks such as MedQA or MedBrowseComp (Lu et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025). For evidence grounding ,we report two claim-level metrics following FActScore (Min et al., 2023) and RAGChecker (Ru et al., 2024). We define Support Rate as the fraction of extracted claims labelled ENTAIL 

by the verifier, and Faithfulness as the fraction of samples with no C ONTRADICT verdict. For behavioural signals we track Tag% (fraction of outputs with a valid <answer>...</answer> 

wrapper) and Search (mean <search> calls per question, surfacing the search-avoidance shortcut in §6). Cross-model generalisation to Llama-3.1-8B and cross-domain transfer to HotpotQA are reported in Appendix 12. 

4.2 Main Results 

The central claim of §1 is that the verifier’s output distribution , not its accuracy, determines whether it provides trainable gradient. We test this by holding the policy, retriever, and reward weights fixed and varying only the NLI back-end. Evaluation runs on Qwen2.5-7B across four held-out medical QA benchmarks (1,479 samples; MEDIQA reported separately in Appendix 9). Table 3 summarises performance averaged across the four benchmarks; Figure 3 shows per-dataset detail. Table 4 positions our setting against closed-frontier baselines on the one dataset where direct comparison is possible. Qwen3-4B + MedNLI-Cls reaches F1 = 0.191, within 0.029 of MedBioLM (0.220, GPT-4o + SFT + RAG) and above the GPT-4o fine-tuning baseline (0.158) by 0.033, de-spite using a ∼10 × smaller backbone with no su-pervised data. The 0.029 gap to MedBioLM does not close the open-vs-closed divide, but is unusu-ally small given ∼10 × less backbone capacity and no supervised fine-tuning. MedNLI-Cls leads BERTScore (0.600) without GPT, while GPT-NLI leads support rate (86.1% vs. 54.0%) at the cost of quality. Likelihood-NLI is competitive on quality (BERT 0.599) but yields zero RL gradient under collapse (§5): its 80.1% support rate is an external GPT-evaluator score on trained outputs, not the training gradient. Per-dataset (Figure 3), MedNLI-Cls leads BERTScore on 3 of 4 sets, losing only on the in-domain Medical split (.575 vs. Likelihood-NLI’s .597); this is con-sistent with the collapse pathway providing format regularisation without GPT co-adaptation (§5.1), which helps most on the training-distribution split. GPT-NLI leads support rate on 3 of 4, losing only on MedicationQA, where Hybrid’s GPT atomic-claim extraction recovers slightly finer-grained ver-dicts (85.2% vs. 84.4%). The trade-off direction is consistent across datasets; the four-dataset average is not driven by any single benchmark. 5 Checker Signal Collapse 

We define checker signal collapse as the condition where an NLI verifier, used as a process reward, assigns a single label to the overwhelming majority of claims regardless of evidence—rendering the reward effectively constant. With Meditron-3-8B log-prob scoring, the checker labels 97–99% of claims neutral, mapping ϕcheck to 0 across rollouts. 

5.1 Calibrated Classifiers Do Not Collapse 

A natural objection is that collapse reflects a poor checker choice. We test this with PubMedBERT fine-tuned on MedNLI (a calibrated classification head 3) on the same claim–evidence pairs. MedNLI-Cls produces non-degenerate verdicts (54.0% sup-port rate averaged across four held-out datasets, vs. 0% for Likelihood-NLI; see also Table 3), local-ising the failure to the scoring mechanism , not to local NLI in general. 

Verification signal vs. format regularisation. 

Collapsed-checker policies still outperform search-only baselines (BERTScore 0.595 vs. 0.537). The mechanism is format regularisation : the auto-check loop forces parseable output (Tag rate 98.9% vs. 58.6% search-only), independent of verdict sig-nal. External GPT-NLI evaluation of these outputs gives 80.1% support rate, confirming the policy 

produces grounded answers; collapse is in the veri-fier’s ability to recognise grounding. This decom-position rules out reward–evaluator co-adaptation (the policy learning to please the GPT evaluator rather than to ground its claims) as the mechanism behind any reported BERTScore gain, including the moderate-signal advantage in § ?? .

6 Reward Hacking Cascade 

Replacing Likelihood-NLI with GPT-NLI resolves signal collapse but does not improve answers; in-stead, the policy discovers a sequence of shortcuts, each countermeasure exposing the next. Stage 1: Ultra-short answers. Average answer length col-lapses from 394 to 130 characters—fewer claims means fewer contradictions. A graded format penalty ( Pfmt = −0.5/ − 0.3/0 for missing tag / 

<50 chars / otherwise) restores length to 216 char-acters. Stage 2: Search avoidance. 98% of sam-ples emit zero search calls, the policy answering from the base model’s internal weights instead of retrieved evidence. A +0 .1 search bonus raises search rate to 96.6% but partially reverts length to 141 characters. Stage 3: Language collapse. Under triage budgets, the policy switches to Chinese to ex-ploit Qwen2.5’s multilingual pretraining; “Always respond in English” in the prompt removes it (Ap-pendix 18). The full sequence demonstrates that strong verification signal under single-pass train-ing is fundamentally unstable; only the moderate MedNLI-Cls signal (§5.1) avoids this pattern. 

RL regularisation cannot rescue this. The α

sweep (App. 26) shows weakening the checker reward to α = 0 .5 still leaves the policy in the strong-signal regime (Support 74.3%) and below MedNLI-Cls quality. Clip and KL penalties con-strain step magnitude, not gradient direction; the cascade is structural , not a tuning artifact. 

7 Discussion 

Our results answer two reviewer-facing concerns. First, the main finding is not circular GPT super-vision: the best configuration, MedNLI-Cls, uses no GPT in the reward path, and answer quality is measured against physician-authored references. Second, reference metrics alone are insufficient for medical RAG: collapsed Likelihood-NLI reaches high BERTScore but 0% training support, showing that answer similarity and evidence-grounded train-ability diverge. The practical implication is to treat checker output distribution as a first-class training diagnostic. 

8 Conclusion 

We compared four NLI checker back-ends as RL process rewards in medical RAG. Three findings refine prior intuitions about verified RL: signal col-lapse is a property of LLM log-probability scor-ing rather than of NLI in general; moderate ver-ification signal outperforms strong signal on an-swer quality due to a reward-hacking cascade; and signal strength is policy-dependent. Practitioners should prefer calibrated classifiers over LLM log-prob scoring for the reward path, and design reward components holistically rather than incrementally. Limitations 

Evaluation independence. Support Rate is com-puted by the same GPT-4o-mini checker used as a training reward component. This parallels standard practice in RL for text generation, where F1 serves as both reward and evaluation metric (Jin et al., 2025). The checker evaluates claim–evidence en-tailment, an objective NLI judgement independent of training dynamics. The wide variation across configurations (0% to 100%) confirms that the met-ric is not trivially optimised. Collapsed-checker configurations receive the same F1 reward but pro-duce zero support tate, showing that the two signals measure distinct properties. 

Single-seed reporting. All reported numbers are from a single training seed per configuration. The sub-percent BERTScore gap between MedNLI-Cls (0.600) and GPT-NLI (0.591) is within plausible evaluation noise on n=1 ,479 samples; we treat the qualitative ordering (MedNLI-Cls ≥ GPT-NLI on quality, GPT-NLI ≥ MedNLI-Cls on support tate) as the robust finding rather than the exact magnitude. The support tate gap (54.0% vs. 86.1%) and the collapse contrast (97% Neutral vs. 54% Entail on the same pairs) are large enough that seed variance is unlikely to flip their sign. 

Test set size. Our in-domain Medical test set contains 87 samples, limiting statistical power on that split alone; held-out coverage (1,479 samples across four out-of-distribution benchmarks) miti-gates this for generalisation claims. 

Limited cross-model coverage. Our primary re-sults use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct with all four checker back-ends. We replicate selected configurations on Qwen3-4B and Llama-3.1-8B (Appendix 12); full back-end replication on these models is left for future work due to compute constraints. The identi-fied failure modes (evidence truncation, non-atomic claim extraction, log-prob bias, reward hacking) are likely model-independent, but language collapse is specific to Qwen’s multilingual pretraining. 

Trade-off between quality and grounding. The MedNLI-Cls checker provides the highest answer quality without GPT dependency, but trails GPT-4o-mini on support tate (54.0% vs. 86.1%). Prac-titioners requiring strict evidence grounding may need GPT API access; those prioritising answer quality or reproducibility can use the fully local configuration. 

Medical domain scope. We conduct all RL train-ing and primary evaluation on medical QA datasets. While the checker itself is domain-agnostic, the RL training dynamics (shortcuts, collapse patterns) may differ in other domains. 

Metric limitations. Token F1 penalises length mismatches between model outputs (200–400 chars) and reference answers (500+ chars). BERTScore is more robust to length variation but introduces its own biases. Human evaluation would strengthen the findings. 

Incomplete cascade resolution. No single con-figuration simultaneously maximises all desirable properties (high F1, high support tate, high search utilisation, stable format). The cascade may con-tain additional undiscovered shortcuts. 

Ethics Statement 

This work uses publicly available medical QA datasets (CSIRO MedRedQA, LiveQA-Med, Pub-MedQA for training; MedicationQA, BioASQ, MedQuAD, MEDIQA for held-out evaluation) that do not contain identifiable patient information. Our system is a research prototype not intended for clinical deployment. Generated medical answers have not been validated by clinical experts and should not be used for health decisions. API calls to GPT-4o-mini are subject to OpenAI’s data usage policies.

13 Retriever Details 

This appendix gives the full specification of the 

<search> tool referenced in §3. The retriever is held constant across every training run and every evaluation result reported in this paper. 

Encoder. We use MedCPT (Jin et al., 2023), a biomedical bi-encoder pretrained on ∼255M PubMed query–article click pairs, with separate query and article towers ( ∼110M parameters each) producing 768-dimensional embeddings. The en-coder is frozen for all RL training; only the policy is updated. 

Corpus. We index the MedRAG corpus (Xiong et al., 2024), which covers four medical sources: • PubMed abstracts: 23.9M entries, title + ab-stract. • StatPearls: 0.3M passages chunked from clin-ical reference articles. • Medical textbooks (the 18 textbooks shipped with the MedRAG release): 0.1M chunks. • Wikipedia medical pages (optional source in MedRAG): enabled. Each source contributes pre-chunked passages of 256 tokens with 32-token overlap; the resulting index contains 26M passage embeddings. 

Index and serving. Embeddings are stored in a FAISS flat inner-product index ( IndexFlatIP ;no quantisation) and served from a single-node HTTP API. Each <search> call sends a UTF-8 query string; the server encodes it with the Med-CPT query tower, runs an exact top-k inner-product search, and returns the top-k = 5 passages with their source identifiers. End-to-end latency is 

<80 ms per call on a single A100. 

Query construction. The agent emits free-form queries between <search> . . . </search> tags. Queries are not rewritten or expanded by the re-triever side; query rewriting, if any, is learned im-plicitly by the policy through GRPO updates. 

Evidence formatting. Returned passages are concatenated in rank order with a short source-tag prefix (e.g. [StatPearls] ) and inserted into the rollout context inside a <information> block. The total injected evidence is truncated to 768 tokens (this truncation is the source of the evidence-fix ablation in §5.2). 

Why hold the retriever fixed. The four checker back-ends compared in §3 can in principle interact with retrieval (e.g., a stronger checker may train the policy to issue more precise queries). To at-tribute quality–grounding differences to checker be-haviour rather than retrieval variation, we share the same encoder, index, top-k, and truncation across all training and evaluation runs. Joint optimisation of the retriever with the policy is left to future work.

17 Multiplicative vs. Additive Reward Coupling 

The reward function in §3.3 couples the checker re-ward multiplicatively rather than additively. When the answer is incorrect ( rbase → 0), ∂R/∂ϕ check →

0, so a high faithfulness score on a wrong answer cannot substitute for correctness. Conversely, when the answer is correct, the multiplier sharpens the gradient toward grounded answers. Additive cou-pling ( R = rbase + α ϕ check ) admits a failure mode in which the policy optimises ϕcheck at the expense of rbase —one face of the cascade we diagnose in §6. 

18 Language Collapse and English-Only Fix 

In the triage-guarded GPT-NLI configuration, 

64.4% of generated answers are in Chinese de-spite English-only training data. The policy ex-ploits Qwen2.5’s multilingual pretraining distribu-tion when triage restricts the turn budget, since Chinese tokens carry comparable F1 reward but avoid checker contradiction risk in the English-conditioned NLI pipeline. When the mixed-language outputs are post-hoc filtered to English-only samples, F1 on that subset reaches 0.214 , the highest single-answer F1 we observed across all configurations—confirming that the model is not failing on the underlying QA task but is exploiting a language-conditional reward gap.

19 Three Root Causes of Log-Prob Collapse 

The body’s collapse summary (§5.2) is supported by a controlled three-step diagnostic chain on the log-prob pathway. Evidence truncation at the default 256-token tool-response limit strips re-trieved passages to 1–2 sentences; extending to 768 tokens raises support rate from 0% to 35.7%. 

Non-atomic claim extraction from sentence split-ting produces multi-proposition claims; replacing with GPT-4o-mini extraction raises support rate to 37.5%. Residual log-prob bias persists even with full evidence and atomic claims, but is largely closed by swapping the scorer (Hybrid back-end: 75.8%; fully GPT-verified: 65.6–77.5%). A small gap to MedNLI-Cls remains in borderline cases where log-prob scoring favours neutral and the clas-sifier commits to entail or contradict.

24 Concurrent Verifier-as-Reward Systems 

The most directly overlapping concurrent system is EvidenceRL (Tamo et al., 2026), which also pairs PubMedBERT-MNLI / MedNLI with GRPO inside a medical RAGagent and reports a grounding-style reward (Table 1). Two differences shape how our results should be read alongside theirs. First, our contribution is not a competing system; it is a di-agnosis of the verifier-as-reward design space. We treat checker output distribution, not checker ac-curacy, as the primary training-time variable, and show that LLM log-probability scoring collapses on the same claim–evidence pairs that a calibrated MedNLI-Cls head scores non-degenerately (§5). Second, we report a three-step reward-hacking cascade that emerges only under strong verifica-tion signal, where an 86.1%-support GPT-4o-mini checker trains a model with lower answer qual-ity than a 54.0%-support MedNLI-Cls checker (§6). Read in this frame, EvidenceRL, VERITAS, and MedRAGChecker occupy specific operating points in the design space: EvidenceRL lands at the moderate-signal regime our cascade analysis predicts is safer; VERITAS reports checker unre-liability without isolating log-probability scoring as the failure mechanism; MedRAGChecker uses claim-level verification at evaluation rather than training time, so the reward hacking cascade does not apply. 25 Predicted Boundary Conditions 

Three conditions are likely to break the findings in § ?? ; we name them so reviewers and follow-up work can test them directly. 

(i) checker strong but uncollapsed. Our cas-cade emerges from the combination of high support rate and on-policy GRPO training without an ex-plicit shortcut-detection step. A checker with com-parable accuracy but more calibration noise (e.g., a sharpened-softmax PubMedBERT fine-tuned to push entailment mass toward 80%) might trigger the cascade even without GPT-class semantics. 

(ii) Different base-policy capacity. Reachabil-ity of cascade shortcuts depends on the policy’s instruction-following capacity. Our Qwen3-4B replication is a partial test: MedNLI-Cls registers as a strong signal on Qwen3-4B (85.3% support, vs. 54.0% on Qwen2.5-7B) yet the cascade end-state does not appear (Tag rate 100%, F1 0.217 matches the Qwen2.5-7B MedNLI-Cls configuration; see Appendix 12). This is consistent with smaller poli-cies having fewer reachable shortcuts under the same reward, and shows that signal strength is a property of the policy–checker pair rather than of the checker alone. A stronger base policy in the 30–70B range may inhabit the opposite regime. 

(iii) Open-domain or non-medical RAG. The benefits we attribute to MedNLI-Cls are domain-specific: in open-domain settings, a generic NLI classifier may collapse where the medical-domain head does not, inverting the moderate-signal advan-tage. We treat these as falsifiable predictions; failing any of (i)–(iii) would localise our findings further rather than refute them.
